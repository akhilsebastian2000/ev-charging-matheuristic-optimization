
introduction:

Electric vehicles (EVs) are no longer a futuristic idea—they are already here, increasing in number every year and placing heavy demand on public charging systems. As more people adopt EVs, charging stations begin to face problems that are very familiar in crowded systems: long queues, unpredictable waiting times, and inefficient use of chargers. Traditional methods like First-Come, First-Serve (FCFS) sound fair, but in real-world scenarios they fail to manage the varying charging needs of vehicles, leading to congestion and wasted time

.

To address this, our project asks a simple, practical question:

“Can we make EV charging smarter by combining data, prediction, and optimization?”

Instead of treating every vehicle and every session the same, we explore a Queue-Aware Matheuristic Framework—a hybrid system that blends machine learning, queueing theory, and intelligent optimization. The idea is to look ahead, estimate how long a vehicle will take, understand how busy the station will be, and then schedule charging in a way that keeps waiting time low and utilization high.

Let’s break down the motivation in a conversational way:

EVs don’t arrive at predictable intervals. Some arrive in clusters, some unexpectedly.

Service times are different for every vehicle. A car with low SOC, low charger power, or high energy demand will naturally take longer to charge.

FCFS doesn’t understand any of this. It simply queues everyone in the order they arrive—which often leads to long delays.

So, why not predict service time using machine learning?
The project uses a trained Random Forest Regressor to estimate charging duration more accurately.

And why stop there?
Once we know the expected service times, we can plug them into a queueing model (M/G/1) and understand how delays build up.

But queueing formulas alone aren’t enough. Real-world queues are messy and uncertain, so the project also uses a Gaussian Process Regression surrogate to approximate waiting times quickly without running expensive simulations.

Finally, how do we choose the best schedule?
That’s where Simulated Annealing, Genetic Algorithms, and Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) come together to search for the best possible charging allocation that minimizes both average waiting time and the 95th percentile delay.

In short, the introduction frames the project as a response to a real, growing challenge:

Charging stations must become smarter, faster, and more adaptive—
and the solution lies in combining prediction + queueing + optimization.

The proposed framework does exactly that. By predicting service times, understanding queue behavior, and optimizing charger assignment, the system aims to reduce waiting times, balance usage across chargers, and ensure a stable, scalable charging operation. Experimental results from this approach show clear improvements over the classical FCFS method, proving that a predictive, queue-aware system can make EV infrastructure significantly more efficient and reliable.



literature survey:
Recent research on EV charging management has moved beyond traditional First-Come, First-Serve (FCFS) approaches due to their inability to handle unpredictable arrivals, heterogeneous charging demands, and long waiting times. Studies increasingly combine predictive modeling, optimization, and data-driven analytics to improve charging efficiency.

Several works integrate machine learning with queueing theory to estimate service durations and waiting times. Pourvaziri et al. applied deep learning to enhance queue prediction accuracy, demonstrating the importance of forecasting service behavior. Other researchers such as Zhao et al. explored multi-level scheduling models that consider diverse user requirements and nonlinear charging profiles.

Metaheuristic optimization has also been widely explored. Evolutionary algorithms, war-strategy optimization, and other heuristic approaches have been used to coordinate EV charging under grid and capacity constraints. While effective for large-scale problems, many of these methods struggle with real-time stochastic arrivals.

More recent work shifts toward reinforcement learning and adaptive scheduling. Deep Q-learning frameworks and dynamic real-time station management strategies show promise in handling demand variability and uncertainty. Additional studies consider economic aspects such as pricing, dynamic admission control, and infrastructure capacity planning.

Research Gap:
Although significant progress has been made, most existing methods lack an integrated framework that combines:

Supervised prediction of service time,

Arrival-rate variability modeling, and

Queue-aware hybrid optimization capable of reducing both average and tail delays.

This gap motivates the development of the proposed Queue-Aware Matheuristic Framework, which unifies machine learning, surrogate queue modeling, and hybrid metaheuristics to achieve reliable, scalable EV charging scheduling.

stage1:

# **Stage 1 — Data Preparation and Service Time Calibration (Elaborated)**

Stage 1 forms the foundation of the entire Queue-Aware Matheuristic Framework. Before any scheduling or optimization can happen, the raw EV session data must be cleaned, structured, and transformed into a form that machine learning models and queueing systems can use reliably. This stage ensures that the service-time predictions—the core input to all later phases—are accurate, stable, and representative of real-world charging behavior.

---

## **1. Understanding the Raw EV Session Data**

The dataset contains session-level information extracted from EV charging logs. Each session records:

* When a vehicle *arrived* at the station
* When charging *started*
* When charging *ended*
* How much energy was drawn
* Charger power rating
* Initial SOC, etc.

All these variables directly influence how long a charging session will take. Since queues form because vehicles need different durations to charge, estimating this duration (service time) is essential for a queue-aware scheduling system.

---

## **2. Computing the Key Targets: Service Time and Waiting Time**

The first objective is to compute two fundamental queue metrics:

### **a. Service Time (Sᵢ)**

Represents how long the EV actually spends charging.

[
S_i = (end_time_i - start_time_i) \times 60
]

This converts the duration into minutes.
Service time depends heavily on **energy drawn** and **charger power**, so capturing its natural variability is important.

### **b. Waiting Time (Wᵢ)**

Represents how long the EV waited before charging began.

[
W_i = (start_time_i - arrival_time_i) \times 60
]

Both Sᵢ and Wᵢ together describe the entire user experience in the charging station and are later used for queue simulation and optimization.

---

## **3. Data Cleaning & Outlier Removal**

The raw dataset may contain inconsistent or unrealistic values—extreme durations, negative timestamps, or very high charging energy values.

To clean the data:

* **Interquartile Range (IQR) filtering** is used.
* Any value outside:
  [
  Q1 - 1.5(IQR) \quad \text{or} \quad Q3 + 1.5(IQR)
  ]
  is treated as an outlier and removed.

This ensures that the model does not learn from abnormal or erroneous sessions that could distort predictions.

---

## **4. Feature Engineering and Scaling**

Machine learning requires inputs in consistent formats. Stage 1 transforms raw features into meaningful numeric vectors.

### **a. Normalization**

Continuous variables like

* *energy_drawn_kwh*
* *charger_power_kw*
  are scaled using **Min–Max normalization**:

[
x' = \frac{x - x_{\min}}{x_{\max} - x_{\min}}
]

This prevents high-magnitude features from overpowering smaller ones during model training.

### **b. Categorical Encoding**

Variables such as:

* *charger type*
* *road condition*
* *session type*

are converted into **one-hot encoded vectors** so the model can understand them numerically.

---

## **5. Correlation Analysis**

Before training any model, the dataset is analyzed to identify relationships between variables.

Using the **Pearson correlation coefficient**:

[
r_{xy} = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2 \sum(y_i - \bar{y})^2}}
]

This helps identify:

* Highly influential predictors
* Multicollinearity among features
* Redundant or weak variables

Early analysis showed a **strong positive correlation** between energy drawn and service time—confirming intuitive behavior.

---

## **6. Training Supervised Regression Models**

The goal is to build a model that can **predict service time Sᵢ** from session features. Two models were evaluated:

### **a. Random Forest Regressor (Primary Model)**

Chosen because it:

* Handles nonlinear relationships
* Manages noise well
* Provides stable predictions

### **b. Gradient Boosting Regressor (Baseline Model)**

Used as a comparison to verify consistency and bias–variance behavior.

### **Feature Vector Used:**

[
x_i = [\text{energy_drawn},; \text{initial SOC},; \text{charger power},; \text{waiting time}]
]

### **Training Objective**

Minimize **Mean Squared Error (MSE)**:

[
MSE = \frac{1}{n}\sum (S_i - \hat{S}_i)^2
]

Models are evaluated using **R² score**, which measures how well predictions match actual values.

**Outcome:**
Random Forest produced predictions clustered tightly around the diagonal line in the Predicted vs Actual plot, proving its reliability.

---

## **7. Feature Importance Insights**

A feature importance analysis revealed that:

* **Energy Drawn (kWh)**
* **Charger Power (kW)**

together explain more than **70% of service-time variance**.

This aligns with physical intuition: more energy + slower charger = longer service time.

---

## **8. Integrating Predictions with FCFS Simulation**

Finally, Stage 1 ends by feeding predicted service times into an **FCFS (First-Come-First-Serve) queue simulator**.

The simulator:

* Uses 6 parallel chargers
* Computes mean waiting time, W95, and utilization
* Provides baseline metrics
* Serves as the reference for all later optimizations

This is crucial because the entire matheuristic framework is evaluated by measuring how much the optimized schedule improves over the FCFS baseline.

---

## **9. Deliverables Produced in Stage 1**

By the end of Stage 1, the following components are completed:

* Cleaned + preprocessed dataset
* Computed waiting and service times
* Trained Random Forest service-time model
* Feature importance analysis
* FCFS baseline simulation results
* Stored models:

  * **service_time_model.pkl** (used in Stage 2)

This avoids overly optimistic scheduling and ensures robustness.

Stage 3 — Queue Surrogate Modeling & Simulation-Based Calibration (Elaborated)

Stage 3 is where the project truly becomes matheuristic—combining mathematical models with heuristic search.
The goal of this stage is simple but powerful:

Build a fast, accurate surrogate model that can estimate queue behavior instantly,
so the optimizer does not need to run expensive simulations at every step.

This stage bridges analytical queue formulas, simulation data, and machine learning to create a surrogate that captures real queue dynamics much faster than traditional methods.

1. Why Stage 3 Is Needed

From Stage 2, we know:

Accurate queue estimates are essential for optimization.

But running Discrete Event Simulations (DES) is slow and computationally expensive.

Heuristic algorithms (SA / GA) need hundreds or thousands of evaluations.

If each evaluation takes seconds or minutes, optimization becomes impossible.

Solution: Build a Surrogate Model

A surrogate model approximates queue performance metrics like:

Mean waiting time (E[W])

95th percentile waiting time (W95)

Utilization (ρ)

Instead of running simulations, the surrogate computes these values in milliseconds.

This is where all the predictive models, queue surrogates, and heuristic techniques from earlier stages come together to form a complete, intelligent scheduling system.

Generate the best possible EV charging schedule that minimizes both average waiting time and tail delays (W95), while respecting charger capacities and maintaining balanced utilization.

Stages 1–3 gave us:

Accurate service-time predictions (Random Forest)

Queue performance estimates (M/G/c theory + GPR surrogate)

A fast evaluation engine for schedule feasibility

But EV charging scheduling is a complex combinatorial optimization problem:

Many vehicles

Multiple chargers

Each with different service times

Under stochastic arrivals

With capacity and utilization constraints

Brute-force or direct MIP is too slow.
Pure heuristic search is inaccurate.
Queue theory alone cannot model dynamic behavior fully.

Stage 4 solves this by blending all methods into a powerful hybrid optimizer.
